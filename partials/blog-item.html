Director Interview

March 24, 2016

In this ongoing series, the blog introduces you some of the finest developers, designers, and support staff in the industry. You’ve already met the developers Nate, Tyler, and Dray; the designers Ayana, Drew, Nick, and John; and our project director, Jennifer. This week, meet Envy’s CFO, Bernie Mapili.

What is your role at Envy?

As the CFO, my primary role is making sure we stay on a healthy financial course. I can’t code nor design but if there’s a business angle to it, I can help. Over the past 5+ years, we’ve built a lifestyle business where people are paid well doing what they love. We’re more than proud of the great clients we get to work for and build kickass things all day long.

The majority of my career has been spent in “Corporate America” where you had to work 60 hours a week to keep your job and brown-nosing superiors was a part of the daily routine, but at least the employees were taken care of with retirement and benefits. It’s incredibly rewarding to find a way to bring the best of old school Corporate America to a company that values home life and healthy work relationships.

What has been the biggest risk you’ve taken to move forward?

The road to self employment has been the most difficult business risk to date. I have the Big Four on my resume, so finding a comfortable spot in the corporate ladder and leading a typical corporate CPA life would have been an easy path to retirement. But it comes at the cost of time with your family when your life consists of long hours and late nights. All my mentors took this route.

For me, the money is not a good enough reason to kill yourself day in and day out and sacrifice family time. I saw an opportunity to go self employed, lost my first house, tapped into savings, and grew a couple businesses to support the life we have today. Since then, I’ve sold the clear winners and losers and kept the businesses that have the highest satisfaction and/or ROI. The number one question I get today is someone asking “should I go self employed?” I respond with three questions for them to answer:

  1. Do you feel a calling to do it as if from God?
  2. Will you sell to anyone and tolerate excessive rejection?
  3. Do you truly have a real product or service and a reasonable plan to profitability?

All it takes is one “no” or “I don’t know” and that’s enough for them to keep praying and plan it out further. Self employment is simply not for the weak or unsure. It’s hard journey with 5–10 years of hard grind up front. But I’d rather do this and enjoy my family for 20+ years, instead of going the way of Corporate America and my mentors of hard grinding every single day until I retire.

Actually work in [your target industry] yourself. That way … the numbers … are more than beans on a table

How would you recommend someone start a career in finance?

The best “first steps” experience is actually operational experience. If somewhere in finance/accounting is your end goal, then the start should be seeing how your targeted industry actually operates on the day-to-day. Actually work in the field yourself. That way once you see the numbers, they are more than beans on a table; each bean comes alive and the numbers are more meaningful. Suddenly it’s not just the boring snoozefest routine revenue you are analyzing. Hey, why is billable revenue so low this month? Before you analyze the beans and dig into individual employee time tracking, think for a moment. Oh, that’s right—most of the Dev team was at Ember Conf in Portland.

What’s different about dealing with finances for a tech company?

From a general sense and core responsibilities, finance and accounting are the same everywhere. Cash go up? Good. Cash go down? Bad. Difficulty comes in random spurts: Is this a recurring everyday expense or are we venturing down the road of R&D? And advising based on the situation.

What is the future of the tech, as you see it?

The next significant leap in internet use will be augmented reality, whether from wearable tech or devices that project an image onto any surface. Imagine a world without a tiny screen on your wrist or a giant smart phone wedged in your skinny jeans just to check your email, text, social media, etc. Actual, true holograms still feel too Star Trek-y to me for now, but interactive projections would be amazing.

Side note: say “no” to skinny jeans.

What was an unexpected experience you had affected your career for the better?

I got my first job in high school when my girlfriend at the time convinced her boss to hire me. Yes, you know why I took the job—to be with her! But the role paid surprisingly great commission, and even though I took the position expecting it to be a dead-end high school job, I ended up learning a lot about sales. Introverted-me learned how to be an extravert, I learned how to convert a cold lead into a sale, and learned how to manage first impressions.

At the end of the day, everyone is selling something.

What’s a life experience you think everyone should have?

Sales. At the end of the day, everyone is selling something, whether it’s direct, passive, actual product, service, their experience, etc. Sure, a used car salesman approach is reprobate, but even if you aim to be a trusted advisor you are selling guidance and knowledge.

For some reason I always wanted to learn how to make eyeglasses, so in college I got a job doing just that. When I was hired, they ended up needing a lot of help on the sales floor, so I did both: I made eyeglasses as well as sold them. Which goes to show: even if you make something yourself, you often still have to sell it.

Side note: I could make a single vision pair in under 15 minutes; for bifocal or progressive it could take me 30 minutes to an hour depending on your RX and frame type!

Owning multiple businesses is great … but my family is my crowning achievement

What life achievement are you most proud of?

Owning multiple businesses is great, and selling Code School for $36 million is definitely a highlight, but my family is my crowning achievement. I won’t launch into a history lesson on my upbringing, but choosing to be home as much as I am is literally an answered prayer. Raising my kids and investing in my marriage is the true American dream (cue the fireworks, American Flags, and jets with colored smoke trails).

What is your favorite place to visit but not live?

Hawaii. Weather seemed better than Orlando and the folks seem more relaxed. But island fever would definitely hit me hard, especially since I love driving on more than three highways and a handful of country back roads.

What would you be doing if you weren’t working in finance?

Assuming bills didn’t have to be paid and family didn’t need food, I would do social work. In my free time I enjoy helping family-friendly charities that operate at 90% or greater efficiency such as The Sunshine Foundation. It’s nice to think that 90¢ out of every $1 you give is actually going to the end participant. Spoken like a true accountant—I know.
